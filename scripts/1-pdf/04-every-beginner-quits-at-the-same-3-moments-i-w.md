**Body:**
I've taught enough total beginners to know exactly where they fall off. The surprise vim screen they can't escape. The wall of text the AI dumps when they needed one line. The silent paralysis of "how do I even paste this into the terminal."

So I stopped treating those as bad luck and started treating them as bugs. Every time someone quit, I wrote down the rule that would have saved them. That file is AUTHORING-PRINCIPLES.md, and it's now eleven entries deep.

P1: never hand them nano or vim — paste-and-run complete blocks, the browser is only for viewing. P11: one short line per turn, like texting a patient friend, not reading a manual. P9: errors are expected and normal — every developer sees them — and debugging IS the skill. Each rule has a real failure behind it.

Here's the part most courses skip: that coaching contract is baked into every module's spec, and a cold-runner — a robot beginner — runs every PDF against all eleven rules before it ships. If a rule can break, the PDF doesn't go out. The proof isn't my promise; it's a gate in the pipeline.

The outcome a learner holds at the end isn't a finished file. It's a filled-in contribution graph and a person who can now direct an AI to build — and never gets stuck on the vim screen again.
